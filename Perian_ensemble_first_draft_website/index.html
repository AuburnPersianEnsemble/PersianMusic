Persian Music Ensemble

Auburn University's Persian Music Ensemble is one credit music course offered by Auburn University Music Department to perform Persian classical and traditional music, including radif and tasnifs (vocal repertoire). Persian Music Ensemble is a group class in which students learn gushe (short melody-type) or a vocal composition of a designated dastgƒÅh (multi-sectional modal structure) phrase by phrase through improvisation. Every Semester, we study and perform major gushes and four vocal compositions within a particular dastgah.

Our study of Persian music begins with the tombak and daff, two percussion instruments, followed by the tar, setar, kamancheh, and santoor (Traditional Persian Instruments). During this class, we introduce right and left hand strokes and gradually work on the primary rhythmic cycles involved in performing and accompanying measured compositions. As a new aspect of music technology in the Persian Music Ensemble, students learn to program different Persian primary rhythmic cycles through EarSketch (Python programming) and Ableton.

Fatemeh Jamshidi

Director, Piano

Fatemeh Jamshidi is a PhD student in computer science and a master's student in music education at Auburn University. She has been a music educator in classical and traditional Persian ensembles since Spring 2017 and the conductor of the Persian Music Group in 2020-2021 at Auburn University. Fatemeh started learning western classical music at the age of six. Her research in combining computer science and music boosted her interest in traditional Persian music. Through her cultural activities at Auburn University, she strives to bring the music of the east to the west to further cross-cultural understanding. She has been training middle school and high school students in computer music for the past five years. She has conducted workshops on teaching programming through music and presented her work at prestigious conferences. Fatemeh's goal is to conduct a music technology center at Auburn University to bridge the gap between computer science and music.

Prashamsa Pandey

Singer

Music has been an integral part of her life since childhood, with playing her ukulele and singing being her preferred mode of self-expression. Dr. Abhishek Jariwala

Percussion / Daf

Dr. Abhishek Jariwala, an esteemed faculty member in Computer Science and Software Engineering at Auburn University, has made a name for himself by blending cutting-edge assistive technology with his passion for the arts. Mike Watkins

Oud

Mike Watkins is a life-long musician and his interest in middle-eastern music began in the early 2000s, studying both Arabic and Persian Music. Mike has played at many venues across the Southeast U.S. including the Gwinnett Center in Atlanta, the Birmingham International Center, and various middle eastern venues in the southeast. Mike has enjoyed playing Persian music with musicians from the U.S. and Iran as well as the music of other middle eastern countries. His favorite Persian musicians are Hossein Alizadeh and Kayhan Kolhor, both of whom he has met at their concerts in Atlanta.

Dr. Ehsan Hassani

Guitar

Dr. Ehsan Hassani was born in Shiraz, Iran. He obtained his bachelor's degree in petroleum engineering and master's in chemical engineering while in Iran. In 2017, he came to the U.S.A. to earn his Ph.D. in chemical engineering. Ehsan was first introduced to guitar at the age of 15 and has continued playing classical persian guitar and persian pop music since then. After coming to America, he quickly connected to the band associated with the Iranian student association's Nowruz celebrations. Since then, he has been a central part of the Nowruz concert band. Eventually he became president of the Iranian Student Association at Auburn University and served the community while keeping the Nowruz band a vital part of the celebrations.
